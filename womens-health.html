What we treat

Symptoms that
deserve proper attention.

Perimenopause and menopause produce a range of symptoms that are frequently dismissed or undertreated in the Canadian healthcare system. They should not be.

Sylven connects you with a physician who has the time to review your situation properly and who is up to date on current evidence-based guidance from the Canadian Menopause Society.

Hot flashes and night sweats

Vasomotor symptoms affect up to 80% of women in the menopausal transition and can significantly disrupt daily life and sleep.

Sleep disruption

Often caused by night sweats and hormonal changes. Chronic sleep deprivation compounds mood and cognitive symptoms.

Vaginal dryness and discomfort

Caused by declining estrogen. Affects intimacy, comfort, and urinary health. Highly treatable with targeted therapy.

Mood changes and anxiety

Hormonal fluctuations affect neurotransmitter activity. Irritability, low mood, and anxiety are physiological responses, not psychological weakness.

Brain fog and concentration

Difficulty with memory and focus is widely reported during perimenopause. Estrogen has documented effects on cognitive function.

Joint pain and fatigue

Estrogen has anti-inflammatory properties. Its decline can cause musculoskeletal discomfort and persistent fatigue that is not explained by other causes.

A note on the evidence for HRT

Hormone replacement therapy was the subject of significant controversy following the 2002 Women's Health Initiative study, which led many physicians to stop prescribing it. Subsequent re-analysis of that data, and twenty years of follow-up research, has substantially revised the picture. For most healthy women in their 40s and 50s who begin HRT within ten years of menopause onset, the benefits for symptom management, bone health, and cardiovascular protection outweigh the risks.

The Canadian Menopause Society and the Menopause Society now support HRT as appropriate first-line treatment for symptomatic women without contraindications. Your physician will review your individual health history and help you make an informed decision.

Who qualifies

Eligibility for
HRT programs.

Most women experiencing perimenopause or menopause symptoms are candidates for HRT. Your physician reviews your individual health history to confirm it is safe and appropriate.

The assessment is free. If your physician determines HRT is not appropriate for you, you receive a full refund of your program fee.

Check Your Eligibility
  • Women experiencing perimenopause or menopause symptoms
  • Women who have had a surgical menopause (oophorectomy)
  • Women 40 and older with qualifying symptoms
  • No personal history of hormone-sensitive cancer (breast, endometrial)
  • No active or recent blood clot (DVT or pulmonary embolism)
  • History of DVT or PE: individual physician risk-benefit review
  • Migraines with aura: transdermal estrogen often appropriate, oral may not be
  • Cardiovascular disease history: individual review required
  • Active or recent breast cancer or endometrial cancer: not eligible
  • Unexplained vaginal bleeding: requires investigation before treatment
  • Known or suspected pregnancy

The concern comes from a 2002 study that has since been substantially revised. That study used older oral synthetic hormones in older women, many of whom already had cardiovascular risk factors. For healthy women in their 40s and 50s starting treatment within ten years of menopause onset, current evidence shows that the benefits of HRT generally outweigh the risks. The Canadian Menopause Society supports HRT as appropriate first-line treatment for symptomatic women without contraindications. Your physician will review your individual situation.

Perimenopause is the transition phase, typically beginning in the mid-40s, during which hormone levels become irregular. Symptoms can begin years before periods stop. Menopause is defined as twelve consecutive months without a period, with an average age of 51 in Canada. You do not need to wait for periods to stop to seek help. If you are experiencing symptoms, a physician can assess you regardless of where you are in the transition.

Women with an intact uterus require progesterone alongside estrogen to protect the uterine lining from overgrowth. This is called combined HRT. Women who have had a hysterectomy can take estrogen alone. Your physician determines which is appropriate based on your history. Formulations vary too: patches and gels deliver estrogen through the skin, which avoids first-pass liver metabolism and may carry a lower clot risk than oral tablets.

Many women notice improvement in vasomotor symptoms such as hot flashes and night sweats within two to four weeks. Sleep often improves soon after. Mood and cognitive symptoms may take six to eight weeks. Vaginal symptoms typically respond within one to three months. Full response to HRT is usually assessed at three months, at which point your physician may adjust formulation or dose.

There is no fixed maximum duration. The Canadian Menopause Society recommends that decisions about duration be made individually, balancing ongoing benefits against any risks, and reviewed regularly with your physician. Many women continue HRT for years. The old guideline of stopping at five years has been largely replaced by an individualised approach based on current evidence.
